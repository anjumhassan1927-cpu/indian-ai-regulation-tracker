# Comparative Jurisdiction Matrix — AI Governance Frameworks

This matrix benchmarks artificial intelligence governance across five jurisdictions:
India, the European Union, the United States, the United Kingdom, and Singapore.
It is maintained as part of the Indian AI Regulation Tracker repository.

---

## 5-Jurisdiction Comparison Table

| Jurisdiction | AI Act / Legislation Status | Liability Framework | Judicial Use Rules | Key Gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **India** | No dedicated AI Act. Governance through IT Act 2000, DPDP Act 2023, and MeitY advisory guidelines. National AI Policy under consultation. | No specific AI liability statute. Tort law and IPC/BNS apply by analogy. Product liability under Consumer Protection Act 2019 partially applicable. | No formal rules on AI use in courts. Some High Courts experimenting with AI tools informally. No Supreme Court directive issued. | Absence of a binding AI liability framework and judicial AI use protocol is the single largest governance gap. |
| **European Union** | EU AI Act 2024 — world's first comprehensive binding AI regulation. Risk-based tiered approach: unacceptable, high, limited, and minimal risk categories. In force August 2024; phased implementation through 2027. | Strict liability proposed for high-risk AI systems under AI Liability Directive (draft). Product Liability Directive revised to cover AI-enabled products. | Explicit prohibition on real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces. High-risk AI in justice administration requires human oversight and auditability. | Implementation timelines create a transitional regulatory gap through 2027. |
| **United States** | No federal AI Act. Executive Order on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI (October 2023). Sectoral regulation by FTC, FDA, and EEOC. State-level laws emerging (Colorado, Illinois). | Common law negligence and products liability. No federal AI-specific tort statute. Algorithmic accountability bills proposed but not enacted. | Individual court rules vary. Federal Judicial Conference monitoring AI use. Evidence admissibility of AI outputs governed by FRE 702 (expert evidence standard). | Fragmented federal-state regulatory landscape creates jurisdictional uncertainty for AI developers and deployers. |
| **United Kingdom** | Pro-innovation, principles-based approach. No single AI Act. AI White Paper (2023) assigns oversight to existing regulators (ICO, CMA, FCA). AI Safety Institute established 2023. | Existing tort, contract, and product liability law applies. Law Commission reviewing AI and automated systems liability. No dedicated statute yet. | Judicial College issued guidance on AI use by judges (2023). Courts and Tribunals AI working group active. AI-generated evidence admissibility under common law review. | Principles-based approach risks regulatory inconsistency across sectors without a unifying statute. |
| **Singapore** | Model AI Governance Framework (2019, updated 2020). AI Verify testing framework (2022). No binding AI Act — voluntary compliance model. Active participant in global AI governance dialogues. | No AI-specific liability law. Contract and tort law apply. Personal Data Protection Act 2012 (amended 2020) covers data-related AI harms. | No formal judicial AI use rules. Singapore Courts digitally advanced but AI adjudication not yet formalised. | Voluntary governance model may prove insufficient as AI deployment in high-stakes sectors accelerates. |

## Policy Commentary

India stands at a critical regulatory inflection point. While the European Union has
established the global benchmark through the EU AI Act 2024 — a binding, risk-tiered
framework covering liability, transparency, and judicial oversight — India's response
remains advisory and fragmented. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 addresses
one dimension of the AI governance challenge but leaves algorithmic accountability,
autonomous decision-making in public administration, and AI use in judicial proceedings
entirely unaddressed.

The comparative picture reveals a clear policy choice India must make: adopt a
principles-based, sector-led model similar to the United Kingdom and Singapore, or move
toward a binding statutory framework modelled on the EU. Given India's constitutional
commitment to the rule of law, its expanding use of AI in public administration and law
enforcement, and the fundamental rights implications of algorithmic decision-making under
Articles 14, 19, and 21 of the Constitution, the case for a binding AI governance
statute — developed through parliamentary deliberation and judicial consultation — is
compelling. The absence of formal judicial AI use rules is particularly urgent: no
institution in Indian democracy is more exposed to the legitimacy risks of unregulated AI
than the courts.

This repository will continue to track legislative developments, judicial pronouncements,
and policy consultations as India's AI governance framework evolves.
