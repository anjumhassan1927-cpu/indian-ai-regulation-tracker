# Indian AI Regulation Tracker — Emerging-Tech Legal Frameworks

Artificial intelligence is reshaping how legal systems govern, adjudicate, and enforce
the law — yet India's regulatory response remains fragmented, rapidly evolving, and
underexplored in legal scholarship. This repository is an open-access, living research
resource that systematically maps the intersection of artificial intelligence, emerging
technology, and law across Indian and comparative jurisdictions.

Maintained by Dr. Anjum Hassan (Ph.D., Law — Amity University, Noida; LL.M. First Class
— GGSIPU, New Delhi), this project extends a line of inter-disciplinary legal research
previously published in Scopus-indexed journals, including work on the regulatory
implications of nanotechnology and law. The tracker applies the same analytical framework
to artificial intelligence — examining how existing Indian statutes, proposed legislative
instruments, and judicial precedents respond to the governance challenges posed by
autonomous systems, algorithmic decision-making, and generative AI.

The repository is structured around four core components. The first is a regulatory
landscape analysis covering the Information Technology Act 2000, the Digital Personal
Data Protection Act 2023, India's draft National AI Policy, and the evolving role of the
Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology. The second is a comparative
jurisdiction matrix mapping AI governance frameworks across the European Union (EU AI Act
2024), the United States, the United Kingdom, Singapore, and India — benchmarking
liability regimes, transparency obligations, and judicial use rules. The third is a series
of annotated case studies applying existing Indian law to emerging AI fact-patterns:
deepfake liability under the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita 2023, algorithmic bias in bail and
sentencing decisions, and the admissibility of AI-generated evidence before Indian courts.
The fourth is a curated research gap index identifying questions that remain unresolved in
Indian legal scholarship — designed to serve as a foundation for future Scopus-targeted
publications and student research projects.

This resource is intended for law faculty, research scholars, and postgraduate students
engaged with technology law, jurisprudence, and constitutional law. It is designed to be
used directly in classroom settings — as a seminar resource, a moot court problem source,
and a platform for collaborative academic inquiry.

Contributions, corrections, and co-authorship proposals are welcome.
