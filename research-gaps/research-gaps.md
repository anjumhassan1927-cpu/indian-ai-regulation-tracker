## Purpose of This Index

This document identifies unresolved questions in Indian AI legal scholarship
that represent priority areas for future peer-reviewed research. Each gap is
framed as a research question suitable for a Scopus-targeted journal article,
doctoral thesis chapter, or postgraduate dissertation. Researchers and scholars
are invited to build on these gaps — see CONTRIBUTING.md for co-authorship
proposals.

---

## Gap 1 — Algorithmic Accountability in Public Administration

### The Problem
Indian public administration increasingly deploys AI and algorithmic tools
in welfare delivery, tax assessment, immigration processing, and social
benefit eligibility decisions. No Indian statute currently requires these
systems to be transparent, explainable, or subject to independent audit.

### Research Question
Does the constitutional guarantee of procedural fairness under Article 21
of the Constitution of India require the state to provide an explanation
of automated administrative decisions that adversely affect individuals,
and if so, what form must that explanation take?

### Why It Matters
The EU AI Act 2024 mandates human oversight and explainability for all
high-risk AI systems used in public administration. India has no equivalent
requirement. With the government's India AI Mission deploying AI across
public services, this gap has immediate constitutional significance.

### Suggested Journals
- National Law School of India Review (Scopus)
- Indian Law Review — Taylor & Francis (Scopus)
- Journal of Indian Law and Society (Scopus)

## Gap 2 — Predictive Policing and Article 21

### The Problem
Several Indian states have deployed or piloted predictive policing tools
that use historical crime data and algorithmic models to identify individuals
as likely future offenders or to allocate police resources to specific
areas. No judicial or legislative framework governs these deployments.

### Research Question
Does the use of predictive policing algorithms by Indian law enforcement
agencies constitute a violation of the right to liberty and dignity under
Article 21, and does it engage the proportionality standard established
in K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)?

### Why It Matters
Predictive policing systems built on historically biased crime data
perpetuate and amplify discrimination. Without a judicial use framework
or statutory safeguard, Indian citizens have no mechanism to challenge
decisions made about them by algorithms they cannot see or contest.

### Suggested Journals
- Asian Journal of Comparative Law (Scopus)
- Criminal Law and Philosophy — Springer (Scopus)
- National Law School of India Review (Scopus)

## Gap 3 — AI-Generated Evidence and Admissibility

### The Problem
Indian courts are beginning to encounter AI-generated content as evidence
— including deepfake videos, AI-reconstructed audio, algorithmically
enhanced images, and outputs from large language models. The Indian
Evidence Act 1872 (now Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023) was not drafted
to address synthetic or AI-generated evidence.

### Research Question
What standard of admissibility should Indian courts apply to AI-generated
evidence, and does the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023 require amendment
to establish a specific foundation requirement for AI-generated content
analogous to the expert evidence standard under Section 45?

### Why It Matters
The reliability of AI-generated evidence is not self-evident — it depends
on the training data, model architecture, and deployment context of the
AI system that produced it. Without a clear admissibility standard, courts
risk either excluding legitimate AI-generated evidence or admitting
unreliable synthetic content that prejudices fair trial rights.

### Suggested Journals
- International Journal of Evidence and Proof — SAGE (Scopus)
- Indian Journal of Law and Technology (Scopus)
- Computer Law and Security Review — Elsevier (Scopus)

## Gap 4 — Platform Liability for AI-Generated Harmful Content

### The Problem
Section 79 of the IT Act 2000 grants intermediaries safe harbour from
liability for third-party content if they act as passive conduits. Generative
AI platforms that produce — rather than merely host — content occupy a
fundamentally different position. No Indian court has yet determined whether
a generative AI platform qualifies as an intermediary under Section 79 or
bears primary liability for harmful content it generates.

### Research Question
Does the Section 79 safe harbour of the IT Act 2000 extend to generative
AI platforms that produce harmful content through their own models, and if
not, what liability standard — negligence, strict liability, or product
liability — should apply under Indian law?

### Why It Matters
This question determines the entire legal architecture of AI content
liability in India. If generative AI platforms can claim Section 79
protection, victims of AI-generated harm — deepfakes, defamatory content,
fraudulent synthetic media — have no viable statutory remedy against the
platform. If they cannot, a new liability framework is urgently needed.

### Suggested Journals
- Computer Law and Security Review — Elsevier (Scopus)
- Journal of Cybersecurity — Oxford (Scopus)
- Indian Law Review — Taylor & Francis (Scopus)

## Gap 5 — AI Regulation and the Right to Equality

### The Problem
AI systems deployed in recruitment, credit scoring, insurance pricing,
and judicial risk assessment frequently produce outcomes that
disproportionately disadvantage women, religious minorities, scheduled
castes, and scheduled tribes — groups protected under Articles 15 and
16 of the Constitution of India.

### Research Question
Does the deployment of AI systems that produce demonstrably discriminatory
outcomes in employment and public services violate Article 15 of the
Constitution of India, and what constitutional remedy is available to
affected individuals in the absence of AI-specific anti-discrimination
legislation?

### Why It Matters
India has no algorithmic anti-discrimination law. The EU AI Act 2024
classifies AI systems used in employment and education as high-risk and
subjects them to mandatory bias auditing. In India, the only recourse is
constitutional litigation — a slow, expensive, and inaccessible remedy
for the communities most likely to be harmed.

### Suggested Journals
- South Asian Human Rights Documentation Centre Review
- International Journal of Discrimination and the Law — SAGE (Scopus)
- National Law School of India Review (Scopus)

## Gap 6 — Children's Rights and AI Recommendation Algorithms

### The Problem
Section 9 of the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 prohibits
behavioural monitoring of children and targeted advertising directed at
minors. AI-driven recommendation algorithms on social media and streaming
platforms routinely do both. No enforcement action has been taken under
Section 9 since the Act received Presidential assent.

### Research Question
Does the Section 9 prohibition on behavioural monitoring of children under
the DPDP Act 2023 effectively constrain AI recommendation algorithms, and
what enforcement mechanisms are necessary to make the prohibition
practically effective?

### Why It Matters
The mental health consequences of algorithmic content amplification for
children are well-documented. India has 600 million internet users under
the age of 25. Without effective enforcement of Section 9, the DPDP Act's
child protection provisions remain aspirational rather than operational.

### Suggested Journals
- International Journal of Law and Information Technology — Oxford (Scopus)
- Child and Family Law Quarterly (Scopus)
- Indian Law Review — Taylor & Francis (Scopus)

## How to Use This Index

**For doctoral researchers**: each gap above contains a framed research
question, a statement of significance, and suggested Scopus-indexed
target journals. These questions are original, unresolved in Indian
legal scholarship, and suitable for full doctoral or postdoctoral
investigation.

**For postgraduate students**: each gap can be narrowed into a
dissertation chapter or seminar paper. The research question as stated
is deliberately broad — a dissertation would typically focus on one
sub-question within the broader gap.

**For law faculty**: these gaps are suitable for designing research
methodology seminars, directing student research projects, and
identifying collaborative research opportunities. See CONTRIBUTING.md
for co-authorship proposals.
